---
title: "Can Words Describe a Single Reality?"
date: 2025-12-11
tags: [philosophy, language, realism, putnam]
summary: "An exploration of Putnam's model theoretic challenge to metaphysical realism."
---

Hilary Putnam, who lived from 1926 to 2016, was an American philosopher known for major contributions to philosophy of language, philosophy of mind, and philosophy of science. In the early 1980s he launched what he called his model theoretic bomb, an argument designed to undermine a view known as metaphysical realism. Metaphysical realism is the belief that there exists a single, fixed, mind independent world and that the truth of a theory is determined by whether its statements correspond to the way the world actually is. Putnam placed himself in the broader category of philosophical anti realists, meaning thinkers who believe that truth and meaning cannot be fully understood as direct correspondence between language and an external world. His bomb argued that metaphysical realism cannot explain how our words latch onto the correct objects in the world. Putnam relied on model theory, which is a branch of mathematical logic that studies the relationship between formal languages and the structures that satisfy their statements. He used these results to show that any consistent theory has many different mathematical interpretations, all of which make the theory come out true. If so, metaphysical realism cannot identify one unique intended interpretation. This failure is what Putnam called his bomb.

To understand Putnam’s argument, one must define what a model is. A model is a structure that assigns objects to words and assigns truth values to statements in a way that makes the theory consistent. According to model theory, there can be many different models for the same set of sentences. For example, suppose we have a simple theory that says cats are mammals and mammals are animals. This theory can be modeled in the real world by the actual biological categories of cats, mammals, and animals. But it can also be modeled mathematically by sets, numbers, or abstract objects that have the same structural relationships. If the structure is preserved, the theory is considered true within that model. Putnam argued that metaphysical realism needs to explain how our language picks out the correct model rather than any of the many mathematically valid alternatives. If a theory fits many models, why should we believe that our words refer to the actual cats, mammals, and animals rather than to sets or numbers arranged in the same pattern. Metaphysical realism assumes that our language has a single correct interpretation, but Putnam claimed that nothing within the theory can select that interpretation.

Putnam illustrated his point with real world examples. Consider the sentence electrons have negative charge. A metaphysical realist would say this sentence is true because electrons in the world actually have negative charge, and the word electrons refers to those particles. Putnam argued that even if the scientific theory is consistent, there could be many mathematically possible interpretations in which the term electrons refers to something else, such as certain sets of abstract objects, as long as the relations defined in the theory remain the same. The realist must explain why our linguistic reference connects specifically to actual electrons and not to any of the other possible models. Putnam concluded that metaphysical realism cannot answer this question. He claimed that the only way to make sense of reference and truth is to adopt internal realism, the view that truth depends on the conceptual scheme we use and that reference is determined by practice, coherence, and usage rather than pure correspondence.

Putnam’s argument drew strong counterarguments. One major response came from metaphysical realists who argued that Putnam confused formal models with genuine physical interpretation. They claimed that model theory shows mathematical possibilities, but the real world has causal and physical features that determine which interpretation is correct. According to this objection, electrons are real objects that enter into causal relations with us, such as producing tracks in detectors. These causal interactions fix the reference of our terms. Another counterargument is that Putnam’s bomb relies on highly artificial reinterpretations of language that no speaker would ever use. Critics argued that reference is grounded in natural language practices rather than in abstract logical models. A third response came from philosophers who defended a form of direct reference, which means that names, natural kind terms, and scientific terms refer directly to things in the world without needing an internal description. On this view, the term electrons refers to electrons simply because that is how it was introduced into scientific practice. These critics claimed that Putnam underestimated the role of historical and social facts in fixing reference.

Despite these objections, Putnam’s model theoretic bomb remains influential because it challenged assumptions about truth, meaning, and realism. It forced philosophers to confront the problem of how language connects to the world and to recognize that logical consistency alone cannot guarantee a unique interpretation. Whether one accepts Putnam’s internal realism or rejects it in favor of metaphysical realism, the bomb sharpened the debate about how humans understand reality. Putnam’s work shows that the relationship between theory and world is more complex than simple correspondence and that philosophical inquiry must take seriously both the structure of language and the practices of science.
